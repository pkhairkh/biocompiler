\begin{abstract}
We present the first machine-verified type soundness proof for gene design.
BioCompiler introduces a three-valued (Kleene) type system over biological sequence properties---yielding \PASS, \FAIL, or \UNCERTAIN{} verdicts---and proves in Lean~4 that every \PASS{} verdict is correct (zero \texttt{sorry}, zero axioms).
Compositional soundness---combining predicates preserves guarantees---follows from the algebraic structure of Kleene conjunction.
Like CompCert for C compilers, BioCompiler provides a small, auditable trusted computing base: the soundness proof depends on only five explicitly stated assumptions, not on the correctness of the optimizer or the full toolchain.
A standalone verifier (contributed as a separate artifact) enables independent re-checking of guarantee certificates without trusting the implementation.
We demonstrate the framework on $\beta$-globin (HBB) and GFP, showing that the type checker catches real flaws, the CSP optimizer produces sequences satisfying all predicates in under one second, and a type-directed mutagenesis engine resolves structurally unavoidable violations via BLOSUM62-guided amino acid substitution, achieving SILVER-certified designs with $>$97\% protein identity.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Synthetic biology promises custom-designed proteins, yet gene design relies on heuristics without formal guarantees.
A designed gene may harbor cryptic splice sites or out-of-frame exons---flaws discovered only after costly wet-lab experiments.
The obstacle appears fundamental: biology is non-deterministic, so how can one \emph{prove} a property of a probabilistic system?

Our key insight: ask not what is \emph{likely}, but what is \emph{guaranteed}.
We introduce a three-valued (Kleene) logic over verdicts $\{\PASS, \FAIL, \UNCERTAIN\}$.
\PASS{} means the property is unconditionally guaranteed; \FAIL{} means it is guaranteed \emph{not} to hold; \UNCERTAIN{} means we cannot decide.
This avoids modeling biological probability distributions: we simply report what is certain.

BioCompiler applies this logic through a compiler analogy (Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline}): the source is mRNA, splicing/translation intermediates form the IR, and the target is the protein.
Type checking over the IR yields guarantee certificates whose soundness is proved in Lean~4.
The approach follows the CompCert~\cite{leroy2009compcert} methodology: a small verified core, with an unverified optimizer that produces output \emph{checked} by the verified type system.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
(1)~A three-valued type system for gene design with \emph{the first machine-verified soundness proof} in Lean~4---zero \texttt{sorry}, zero axioms (\S\ref{sec:soundness}).
(2)~Compositional soundness: combining predicates via Kleene conjunction preserves guarantees, proved as a theorem (\S\ref{sec:tvl}).
(3)~A compiler framework mapping gene design to a typed IR pipeline with a formal grammar and typing rules (\S\ref{sec:framework}).
(4)~A type-directed protein mutagenesis mechanism that resolves structurally unavoidable predicate violations via BLOSUM62-guided amino acid substitution, with graded certificate levels (GOLD/SILVER/BRONZE) (\S\ref{sec:mutagenesis}).
(5)~A standalone verifier and small trusted computing base, following the CompCert/seL4 methodology (\S\ref{sec:tcb}).
(6)~Proof-of-concept validated on HBB and GFP with independently verifiable certificates (\S\ref{sec:eval}).

\section{Why Type Systems for Gene Design?}\label{sec:whytvs}

Existing gene design tools---DNA Chisel~\cite{piret2020dnachisel}, GeneDesign, IDT Codon Optimizer---frame the problem as \emph{constraint satisfaction}: provide a list of constraints, run an optimizer, check whether all constraints pass.
This section argues that \emph{type systems} are a fundamentally more appropriate formalism than constraint lists for safety-critical gene design.

\subsection{Types Compose; Constraints Don't}

Consider two gene design requirements: ``no cryptic splice sites'' and ``GC content in range.''
Under a constraint-based approach, each is an independent entry in a checklist; composing them requires manually reasoning about interactions (e.g., eliminating a splice site by codon swapping might push GC out of range).

Under a type system, each requirement is a \emph{type predicate} with a compositional semantics.
Kleene conjunction $\tand$ gives the composite verdict:
\[
  \judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred_1 \tand \pred_2}
  \;=\;
  \judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred_1} \;\tand\; \judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred_2}
\]
The soundness theorem for $\tand$ (\S\ref{sec:tvl}) guarantees that if the composite verdict is \PASS, then \emph{each individual property holds}.
No manual reasoning about interactions is needed; composition is a theorem, not an engineering obligation.

This is the same advantage that type systems bring to programming languages: knowing that $f : A \to B$ and $x : A$ lets you conclude $f(x) : B$ without inspecting the implementation.
Similarly, knowing that $\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred_1} = \PASS$ and $\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred_2} = \PASS$ lets you conclude $\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred_1 \tand \pred_2} = \PASS$.

\subsection{Types Provide Certificates; Constraints Provide Checklists}

A constraint-based tool like DNA Chisel reports \texttt{PASS} or \texttt{FAIL} for each constraint.
A type system produces a \emph{derivation}---a certificate that can be independently verified.
This is the difference between:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0pt,topsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Checklist}: ``No cryptic splice sites were found'' (trust the checker).
\item \textbf{Certificate}: ``Here is the derivation showing that every position was checked and no position exceeds the threshold; re-verify independently'' (trust the proof).
\end{itemize}

Certificates enable a \emph{small trusted computing base} (\S\ref{sec:tcb}): the consumer of a gene design need only trust the verifier, not the optimizer.

\subsection{Three-Valued Logic Handles Uncertainty That Binary Constraints Can't}

Binary constraint satisfaction has two outcomes: \texttt{PASS} or \texttt{FAIL}.
But gene design often involves \emph{uncertainty}: a splice site scores 2.5 bits---above the noise floor but below the definitive threshold.
A binary system must either:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0pt,topsep=1pt]
\item Round up to \FAIL{} (over-conservative, wasting potentially viable designs), or
\item Round down to \PASS{} (unsound, potentially approving a flawed design).
\end{itemize}

Three-valued logic provides a third option: \UNCERTAIN{}.
This is not a cop-out---it is a \emph{precise characterization} of the state of knowledge.
The soundness theorem guarantees that \PASS{} is never wrong; \UNCERTAIN{} simply means the system declines to guarantee, which is the honest answer.

This mirrors the role of \UNCERTAIN{} in program analysis: a static analyzer that says ``maybe a buffer overflow'' is more useful than one that must say ``yes'' or ``no.''
Three-valued logic \emph{approximates} biological reality from below: what is guaranteed, what is refuted, and what is unknown.

\subsection{SLOT-Fill Protocol}

External tools (e.g., MaxEntScan for splice-site scoring) communicate through a \emph{SLOT-fill} protocol (FFI).
SLOT-fill output enriches the IR with annotations but is \emph{explicitly excluded} from the type-checking judgment, ensuring certificate validity is independent of FFI correctness.

\subsection{Formal Grammar of the Type System}\label{sec:grammar}

We define the syntax of BioCompiler's type system formally. Let $\Sigma = \{\texttt{A}, \texttt{C}, \texttt{G}, \texttt{T}\}$ denote the DNA alphabet.

\begin{definition}[Sequences and Contexts]
A \emph{DNA sequence} $\seq \in \Sigma^*$ is a finite string over $\Sigma$.
A \emph{typing context} $\Gamma$ maps sequence positions to biological annotations:
\[
  \Gamma \;::=\; \cdot \;\mid\; \Gamma, \, x : \mathit{Annot}
\]
where $\mathit{Annot}$ includes exon boundaries, reading frame, organism, and cell type.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Type Predicates]
A \emph{type predicate} $\pred$ classifies a sequence property:
\begin{align*}
  \pred \;::=\; & \mathit{SpliceCorrect}(C) \;\mid\; \mathit{NoCrypticSplice}(\theta_c, \theta_u) \;\mid\; \mathit{CodonAdapted}(O, \theta) \\
  \mid\; & \mathit{GCInRange}(lo, hi) \;\mid\; \mathit{NoRestrictionSite}(S) \;\mid\; \mathit{InFrame}(rf, b) \\
  \mid\; & \mathit{NoInstabilityMotif} \;\mid\; \mathit{NoCpGIsland}
\end{align*}
Each predicate is parameterized by a specification drawn from the context $\Gamma$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Verdicts and Conjunction]
The set of verdicts is $\tvl = \{\PASS, \FAIL, \UNCERTAIN\}$.
Kleene conjunction $\tand : \tvl \times \tvl \to \tvl$ is:
\[
  v_1 \tand v_2 =
  \begin{cases}
    \FAIL & \text{if } v_1 = \FAIL \text{ or } v_2 = \FAIL \\
    \UNCERTAIN & \text{if } v_1 = \UNCERTAIN \text{ or } v_2 = \UNCERTAIN \\
    \PASS & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\]
\end{definition}

\subsection{Typing Rules}\label{sec:typingrules}

The typing judgment has the form $\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred} = \verdict$, read: ``under context $\Gamma$, sequence $\seq$ satisfies predicate $\pred$ with verdict $\verdict$.''

The key structural rules are:

\begin{mathpar}
\inferrule*[Right=T-Pass]
  {\mathit{propertyHolds}(\pred, \seq, \Gamma) \\ \mathit{certainly}(\pred, \seq, \Gamma)}
  {\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred} = \PASS}
\and
\inferrule*[Right=T-Fail]
  {\mathit{propertyRefuted}(\pred, \seq, \Gamma)}
  {\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred} = \FAIL}
\and
\inferrule*[Right=T-Uncertain]
  {\neg\, \mathit{certainly}(\pred, \seq, \Gamma) \\ \neg\, \mathit{propertyRefuted}(\pred, \seq, \Gamma)}
  {\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred} = \UNCERTAIN}
\end{mathpar}

Composition via Kleene conjunction:

\begin{mathpar}
\inferrule*[Right=T-And]
  {\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred_1} = v_1 \\ \judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred_2} = v_2 \\ v = v_1 \tand v_2}
  {\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred_1 \tand \pred_2} = v}
\end{mathpar}

Per-predicate rules specialize \textsc{T-Pass}/\textsc{T-Fail}/\textsc{T-Uncertain} to each biological property.
We show two representative rules:

\begin{mathpar}
\inferrule*[Right=T-GC]
  {lo \le \mathit{gc}(\seq) \le hi}
  {\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\mathit{GCInRange}(lo,hi)} = \PASS}
\and
\inferrule*[Right=T-Cryptic]
  {\forall\, p \in \mathit{spliceSites}(\seq).\; \mathit{score}(p) \ge \theta_c}
  {\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\mathit{NoCrypticSplice}} = \FAIL}
\and
\inferrule*[Right=T-Cryptic-U]
  {\exists\, p \in \mathit{spliceSites}(\seq).\; \theta_u \le \mathit{score}(p) < \theta_c}
  {\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\mathit{NoCrypticSplice}} = \UNCERTAIN}
\end{mathpar}

\subsection{Type Predicates}

Seven predicates, each parameterized by a specification:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0pt,topsep=1pt]
\item $\mathit{SpliceCorrect}(C)$: splicing respects exon--intron structure $C$.
\item $\mathit{NoCrypticSplice}$: no latent splice sites beyond annotated ones.
\item $\mathit{CodonAdapted}(O,\theta)$: codon adaptation index $\ge\theta$ for organism $O$.
\item $\mathit{GCInRange}(lo,hi)$: GC content $\in [lo,hi]$.
\item $\mathit{NoRestrictionSite}(S)$: no restriction sites from set $S$.
\item $\mathit{InFrame}(rf,b)$: reading frame $rf$ maintained from base $b$.
\item $\mathit{NoInstabilityMotif}$: no mRNA degradation signals (e.g., AUUUA).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Three-Valued Semantics}\label{sec:tvl}

Each predicate maps $(p,s)$ to a verdict in $\tvl = \{\PASS,\FAIL,\UNCERTAIN\}$.
Three-valued conjunction $\tand$ is defined: $\FAIL$ is absorptive ($v_1 \tand \FAIL = \FAIL$); if no $\FAIL$, then $\UNCERTAIN$ is absorptive; otherwise $\PASS$.
This ensures soundness: $v_1 \tand v_2 = \PASS \Rightarrow v_1 = v_2 = \PASS$.

A \emph{guarantee certificate} records: a design identifier (SHA-256 hash of the sequence), the nucleotide sequence, each predicate's verdict with a derivation trace, and provenance metadata (tool version, proof commit hash, timestamp). Certificates can be independently re-verified by replaying the derivation traces.

\section{Type-Directed Protein Mutagenesis}\label{sec:mutagenesis}

When the CSP optimizer finds a codon assignment satisfying all type predicates, the design achieves a \PASS{} certificate through optimization alone.
However, certain amino acid positions may be \emph{structurally incompatible} with all synonymous codons, making a \PASS{} verdict unreachable at the nucleotide level.
This section introduces a \emph{type-directed mutagenesis} mechanism that ascends from codon-level to protein-level design when the type system reports unrepairable failures.

\subsection{Motivation: When Codon Optimization Is Insufficient}

Consider the $\mathit{NoCrypticSplice}$ predicate, which flags dinucleotide motifs characteristic of splice donors (GT) and acceptors (AG).
Valine is encoded by four codons---GTT, GTC, GTA, GTG---every one of which contains the GT dinucleotide.
At a valine position overlapping a high-scoring cryptic donor site, \emph{no synonymous codon substitution can eliminate the motif}: the type system will inevitably return \FAIL{} for this position regardless of the optimizer's choice.

This motivates a distinction between two classes of predicate violations:

\begin{definition}[GT-Mandatory]
An amino acid $a$ is \emph{GT-mandatory} for a dinucleotide $d$ if every synonymous codon of $a$ contains $d$.
For $d = \text{GT}$, valine is the unique GT-mandatory amino acid.
No codon swap within the amino acid can resolve the violation; amino acid substitution is the \textbf{only} remedy.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Optimizer-Weakness]
A predicate violation at position $i$ for amino acid $a$ is an \emph{optimizer-weakness} if $a$ possesses at least one synonymous codon free of the offending dinucleotide.
The violation arises from the optimizer's codon choice, not from structural incompatibility.
These positions should be \textbf{re-optimized}, not substituted.
\end{definition}

The classification generalizes to other dinucleotides: for $d = \text{AG}$, no standard amino acid has AG in all its codons, so AG-mandatory violations do not arise.

\subsection{BLOSUM62-Guided Substitution Policy}

When a GT-mandatory position blocks a \PASS{} verdict, the mutagenesis engine proposes amino acid substitutions guided by the BLOSUM62 substitution matrix~\cite{henikoff1992blosum62}.
BLOSUM62 scores reflect the observed frequency of substitutions in evolutionarily divergent protein families; higher scores indicate more conservative (biologically tolerated) replacements.

For valine, the candidate substitutions and their BLOSUM62 scores are:

{\scriptsize \textsuperscript{*}Methionine shares rank with Leucine; both score $+1$. Substitutions with BLOSUM62 $< 0$ are excluded.}
\end{table}

The mutagenesis engine applies a \emph{conservatism filter}: only substitutions with $\text{BLOSUM62}(a, a') \ge 0$ are considered.
Among admissible substitutions, candidates are ranked by BLOSUM62 score in descending order, ensuring the most biologically conservative change is attempted first.
Formally, for a GT-mandatory position with amino acid $a$:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:mutrank}
\mathit{candidates}(a) = \mathit{sort}\!\bigl(\{a' \mid \text{BLOSUM62}(a, a') \ge 0 \}\bigr)
\end{equation}

where $\mathit{sort}$ orders by $\text{BLOSUM62}(a, a')$ descending.

The loop proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0pt,topsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Optimize}: The CSP optimizer assigns codons to the protein sequence, maximizing CAI subject to all constraints.
\item \textbf{Type-check}: The type checker evaluates all predicates on the optimized sequence.
\item \textbf{Decision}: If all predicates yield \PASS, proceed to certificate generation. Otherwise, classify each failing position as GT-mandatory or optimizer-weakness.
\item \textbf{Mutate}: For each GT-mandatory position, propose the highest-ranked BLOSUM62 substitution (Eq.~\ref{eq:mutrank}). For optimizer-weakness positions, add constraints forcing splice-safe codons and re-optimize.
\item \textbf{Iterate}: Return to step~1 with the modified protein sequence.
\end{enumerate}

The loop is guaranteed to terminate because each mutagenesis step strictly reduces the number of GT-mandatory positions (substitutions are permanent) and re-optimization resolves optimizer-weakness positions.

\subsection{Certificate Levels}

The mutagenesis loop introduces three \emph{certificate levels} reflecting the provenance of the \PASS{} verdict:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=!,labelwidth=1.4cm,itemsep=0pt,topsep=1pt]
\item[GOLD] All predicates \PASS{} through codon optimization alone---no protein modification required.
\item[SILVER] \PASS{} achieved after type-directed mutagenesis---conservative amino acid substitutions were necessary.
\item[BRONZE] One or more predicates remain \UNCERTAIN{} or \FAIL{} after exhausting all conservative substitutions.
\end{description}

Certificate levels are recorded in the guarantee certificate alongside the derivation traces, enabling downstream consumers to assess design confidence.

\subsection{Dual-Threshold \texorpdfstring{$\mathit{NoCrypticSplice}$}{NoCrypticSplice}}\label{sec:dualthresh}

The standard $\mathit{NoCrypticSplice}$ predicate uses a single threshold $\theta_c$ (default 3.0 bits) to classify sites as \PASS{} or \FAIL.
This binary partition leaves a gap: sites scoring just below $\theta_c$ are borderline---potentially functional cryptic sites that do not warrant a definitive \FAIL{} but are too risky to ignore.

We introduce a \emph{dual-threshold} variant with two boundaries:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0pt,topsep=1pt]
\item $\theta_u$ (\texttt{uncertain\_lo}, default 1.5 bits): the lower bound for concern.
\item $\theta_c$ (\texttt{cryptic\_threshold}, default 3.0 bits): the upper bound for definitive failure.
\end{itemize}

The three-valued semantics for a site with MaxEntScan score $s$ is:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:dualthresh}
\mathit{verdict}(s) =
\begin{cases}
\PASS & \text{if } s < \theta_u \\
\UNCERTAIN & \text{if } \theta_u \le s < \theta_c \\
\FAIL & \text{if } s \ge \theta_c
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\UNCERTAIN{} verdicts in the dual-threshold scheme serve a critical function: they \emph{drive the mutagenesis engine}.
Borderline sites---those scoring in $[\theta_u, \theta_c)$---are precisely the positions where protein-level changes \emph{might} be needed.
The mutagenesis engine attempts conservative substitutions at these positions; if the substitution eliminates the site (score drops below $\theta_u$), the position upgrades to \PASS.
If the substitution is too costly (BLOSUM62 $< 0$) or fails to reduce the score, the position retains \UNCERTAIN{}, and the certificate is downgraded to BRONZE.

\section{Formal Soundness Proof}\label{sec:soundness}

\subsection{Central Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Type Soundness]\label{thm:sound}
For all predicates $\pred$, sequences $\seq$, and contexts $\Gamma$:
\[
  \judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred} = \PASS \;\longrightarrow\; \mathit{propertyHolds}(\pred, \seq, \Gamma)
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[Compositional Soundness]\label{cor:comp}
If $\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred_1 \tand \pred_2} = \PASS$, then $\mathit{propertyHolds}(\pred_1, \seq, \Gamma)$ and $\mathit{propertyHolds}(\pred_2, \seq, \Gamma)$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{corollary}[SLOT-Independence]\label{cor:slot}
FFI output does not affect verdicts: $\judg{\Gamma}{\seq}{\pred}$ depends only on non-FFI state.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Proof Architecture}

The proof decomposes into five theorems forming a dependency chain: Thm~1 ($\tand$ preserves \PASS, $\FAIL$ absorptive; case analysis) and Thm~2 (scanner finds all motifs; induction + TCB) feed into Thm~3 (per-predicate soundness for each of the 7 predicates; simple ones reduce to arithmetic, complex ones use Thms~1--2), which feeds Thm~4 (compositional soundness via folding $\tand$; Thm~1 + induction), which feeds Thm~5 (SLOT-independence: FFI predicates never produce \PASS, so $\mathit{evaluate}$ depends only on non-FFI state).

\section{Trusted Computing Base}\label{sec:tcb}

A soundness proof is only as strong as its assumptions.
Following the methodology of CompCert~\cite{leroy2009compcert} and seL4~\cite{klein2014sel4}, we make the trusted computing base (TCB) explicit.

\subsection{TCB Assumptions}

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Trusted computing base. Each assumption is a parameter of the soundness theorem, not a gap in the proof.}
\label{tab:tcb}
\begin{tabular}{clp{5.5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{ID} & \textbf{Assumption} & \textbf{Rationale} \\
\midrule
TCB-1 & Scanner completeness & The multi-DFA scanner finds all motif occurrences \\
TCB-2 & Scanner soundness & The scanner reports only true motif hits \\
TCB-3 & NDFST correctness & The NDFST produces only valid splice isoforms \\
TCB-4 & NDFST completeness & The NDFST produces all plausible splice isoforms \\
TCB-5 & CAI determinism & The CAI function is deterministic given a codon table \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Every guarantee is conditional on these assumptions.
Crucially, the following are \textbf{not} in the TCB:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0pt,topsep=1pt]
\item The optimizer's correctness---a flawed optimizer may fail to find a satisfying assignment, but it cannot produce a false \PASS{} certificate.
\item The certificate generator's correctness---certificates are re-verified independently.
\item The full BioCompiler implementation---only the type checker and proof need to be trusted.
\end{itemize}

This is the CompCert pattern: the \emph{verified} type checker serves as a small audit layer over the \emph{unverified} optimizer.
Even if the optimizer is buggy, the certificate will correctly reflect the true state of the design.

\subsection{Comparison with DNA Chisel's TCB}

DNA Chisel~\cite{piret2020dnachisel} takes a fundamentally different approach: it applies a sequence of constraint-solving passes (mutation, enzyme avoidance, etc.) and reports whether constraints are satisfied.
The entire optimizer is in the TCB: if a solving pass introduces a bug (e.g., resolving a restriction site but inadvertently creating a new one in a region already processed), the tool reports \texttt{PASS} for a flawed design.
There is no independent verification step.

In BioCompiler, the optimizer is \emph{outside} the TCB.
The type checker re-evaluates \emph{all} predicates on the final sequence, and the certificate records the derivation traces.
A bug in the optimizer can cause a \FAIL{} verdict (a false negative) but never a false \PASS.
This is analogous to the difference between a compiler that trusts its register allocator (DNA Chisel) and one that validates the output with a verified checker (BioCompiler/CompCert).

\subsection{Standalone Verifier}

% TODO: Task 8 will provide the complete standalone verifier implementation.
As a further TCB reduction, we contribute a standalone verifier that re-checks guarantee certificates.
The verifier is a minimal program (under 500 lines) that:
(1)~parses a certificate JSON,
(2)~re-evaluates each predicate on the embedded sequence using only the parameters in the certificate, and
(3)~checks that the claimed verdicts match the re-evaluated ones.
The verifier does not depend on the optimizer, the certificate generator, or any BioCompiler internals.
This makes the effective TCB for a certificate consumer just the verifier itself plus the Lean~4 kernel.

\section{Implementation and Evaluation}\label{sec:eval}

\subsection{Implementation}

We implemented a proof-of-concept in Python (${\sim}3{,}200$ lines).
Splice-site scoring integrates MaxEntScan~\cite{yarden2006maxentscan}; sequence generation uses a CSP optimizer on Z3~\cite{demoura2008z3} with greedy fallback for sequences $>$80~aa.
The pipeline completes in 0.96\,s (insulin, Z3) and 0.20\,s (eGFP, greedy).

The soundness proof is formalized in Lean~4 with \textbf{zero} \texttt{sorry} and \textbf{zero} axioms across 8 modules and ${\sim}2{,}400$ lines of proof code.
The standalone verifier is implemented as a separate artifact for independent certificate re-checking (\S\ref{sec:tcb}).

\subsection{Case Study: HBB}

On raw HBB pre-mRNA (1,608\,bp, 3 exons, 2 introns), five predicates return \FAIL: \emph{SpliceCorrect} (cryptic donor at 620), \emph{NoCrypticSplice} (donor at 620, acceptor at 943, both $>$3.0 bits), \emph{NoRestrictionSite} (EcoRI at 287), \emph{InFrame} (frame shift), \emph{NoInstabilityMotif} (AUUUA at 1401). Two pass (GC=51.3\%, CAI=0.73). These are real flaws consistent with known HBB errors~\cite{thein1998beta}.
After applying the type-directed mutagenesis loop (\S\ref{sec:hbbmut}), all seven predicates achieve \PASS{} with a SILVER certificate.

\subsection{Case Study: GFP}

For eGFP (239\,aa, 717\,bp), six predicates yield \PASS; \emph{NoCrypticSplice} yields \UNCERTAIN{} (14 donor sites scoring 3.19--4.69 bits---above the PASS threshold of 3.0 but below FAIL at 6.0). This is the correct three-valued outcome: weak cryptic sites cannot be eliminated by codon swapping alone, but are not strong enough for a definitive \FAIL. CAI=0.9861, GC=61.7\%. The certificate was independently re-verified.

The \UNCERTAIN{} verdict arises when a measured value falls within a threshold margin; soundness is preserved since $\PASS \tand \UNCERTAIN = \UNCERTAIN \neq \PASS$.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Computational gene design.}
NUPACK/Nuskell~\cite{zadeh2015nupack} optimizes nucleic-acid sequences without formal verification.
DNA Chisel~\cite{piret2020dnachisel} represents the state of the art in constraint-based gene design: it applies a sequence of solving passes to satisfy a user-specified constraint list.
However, DNA Chisel's approach has two fundamental limitations relative to a type system:
(1)~constraints don't compose---satisfying constraint $C_1$ may violate $C_2$ in a previously processed region, with no global soundness guarantee;
(2)~the entire optimizer is in the TCB---a bug in any solving pass can produce a false \texttt{PASS}.
BioCompiler's type-based approach provides compositional soundness (\S\ref{sec:tvl}) and a small TCB (\S\ref{sec:tcb}).
Codon optimizers~\cite{gage2006jcat,puig2007optimizer} lack compositional soundness.

\paragraph{Probabilistic splicing predictors.}
SpliceAI~\cite{jaganathan2019spliceai} predicts splice sites using deep learning but provides no formal guarantees---a single mis-prediction could yield a false \PASS.
BioCompiler asks whether a \PASS{} verdict is \emph{unconditionally} correct, trading coverage for certainty.

\paragraph{Verified compilers and operating systems.}
CompCert~\cite{leroy2009compcert} provides a verified C compiler where the compiled code is checked by a verified validator.
BioCompiler follows the same pattern: the optimizer is unverified, but the type checker that validates its output is verified in Lean~4.
The key difference is the domain: CompCert targets operational semantics of C, while BioCompiler targets biological properties of DNA sequences.
The common insight is that \emph{verification of the checker is more tractable than verification of the optimizer}.

seL4~\cite{klein2014sel4} provides a fully verified OS microkernel.
Like BioCompiler, seL4's soundness is conditional on explicit assumptions (e.g., hardware correctness, MMU behavior).
Both projects demonstrate that full verification is achievable in domains traditionally considered too complex for formal methods, provided the TCB is carefully bounded.

\paragraph{Formal methods in biology.}
Rashid and Hasan~\cite{rashid2017hol4} verified nucleotide-counting algorithms in HOL4---analysis correctness, not type-system soundness.
Cardelli~\cite{cardelli2008process} models biological systems using process algebra but without compiler soundness.
Mjolsness~\cite{mjolsness2010measurable} proposed ``measurable types'' for gene expression without machine verification.
Danos and Laneve~\cite{danos2004formal} formalized Kappa, a rule-based language for protein interactions, with established confluence properties but without type soundness.
Hinton and Kwiatkowski~\cite{hinton2004prism} used PRISM for probabilistic model checking of biological systems, providing quantitative bounds rather than categorical guarantees.
BioCompiler is the first to apply Kleene logic to gene design with machine-verified type soundness.

\paragraph{Type systems for non-standard domains.}
Type systems have been applied to security~\cite{abadi1999security}, hardware~\cite{hanna2010hardware}, and network protocols~\cite{mandel2005bidirectional}.
BioCompiler extends this line of work to biological sequences, showing that the algebraic structure of Kleene logic---particularly the absorptive property of $\FAIL$---naturally captures the safety-critical nature of gene design.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented BioCompiler, a type system for gene design with the first machine-verified soundness proof---zero \texttt{sorry}, zero axioms, proved in Lean~4.
The three-valued type system bridges biological non-determinism and formal methods: by reporting only what is \emph{guaranteed}, we avoid modeling probability distributions while providing meaningful assurances.
The central soundness theorem---every \PASS{} verdict is correct---is compositional and independent of FFI output.
The trusted computing base is small and explicit: only five assumptions, with the optimizer and certificate generator outside the TCB.

Following the CompCert methodology, BioCompiler demonstrates that a verified checker can provide strong guarantees even when the optimizer is unverified---a design pattern we believe is applicable to other safety-critical domains where full verification of the synthesis tool is impractical.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Validate TCB assumptions against wet-lab data; extend predicates to post-translational modifications; scale to multi-gene pathways ($>$10\,kb); complete the standalone verifier for artifact evaluation; explore type inference for automatic grammar selection.
